Owning and maintaining a heritage building can be quitetricky if you’re not in the know. One thing that homeowners struggle with isfinding reliable tradesmen who have a good understanding of the correctmaterials. Alongside the use of synthetic insulation and plastic coatings, oneof the biggest concerns is the use of cement mortars instead of lime mortars. 

Although there were instances of earlier use, modern cement didn’tcome to prevalence in the UK until the post-WWII boom and was considered awonder material at the time because of its strength and fast setting times, comparedto traditional lime mortars. It’s only in the past 20-or-so years that buildingconservation professionals are understanding the impact that hard modern cement,and by extension other modern materials such as silicone renders, have onadjacent and underlying masonry. The problem with this is that now the majorityof tradesmen have worked with cement, their fathers and their grandfathers usedit, they have ’40 years experience’ using it and are unaware of the newresearch being done. Cement based products are cheap, easy to work with andeasy to get hold of so it’s no wonder builders insist on using them. 

This is compounded by an education system that focusses onbuilding new houses instead of correctly retrofitting existing vacant units;something which I believe is sorely needed in the UK. We have the oldesthousing stock in Europe and it’s mostly Victorian and Edwardian terraces thanksto  the UK being the heart of theindustrial revolution.

This is by no means a post to tear down your averagetradesperson – I can’t imagine anyone wants to put in a full day’s hard graftto then come home and read the latest research paper by Historic England or theSPAB. The intention of this post is to draw a homeowner’s attention to thewhere issues might occur if we don’t understand the technicalities of oldbuildings, so they can better advocate for their buildings when discussing theirproject.

Cement is a great material to use on modern buildings thatdo not need to breath, have movement joints built in and have cavities and DPCsto prevent moisture getting into the building. They then have extract fansinternally to get moisture out. However, I’m such a massive advocate for limethat I’d suggest using it on new buildings as well – you won’t need thoseunsightly movement joints and it’s so much better for the environment as a hydrauliclime sets by taking carbon out of the air!

So how do you know if your building is pointed or renderedwith lime or cement?

The best thing to do is to take a sample and get it testedprofessionally – there are a number of companies online and you’ll get a betterunderstanding of what the mortar mix is made up of and can try to replicate itlike-for-like. This will ensure the mortar or render is technically appropriatefor your building and it will give you a better colour match. Alternatively,you can put some of your mortar in a small dish of white vinegar at home. Ifthe mortar fizzes then this is the alkaline lime reacting with the acidicvinegar – cement wouldn’t have the same reaction.

But what if my old building has already been repointed orrendered with modern cement?

It can be repaired, and you will likely have some of theoriginal mortar deeper within the mortar joint that can be removed and tested.Just ensure that any cement mortar is removed carefully to prevent damage to brickwork.

But my building isn’t listed.

A humble two-up-two-down Victorian workers terrace doesn’tknow it’s listed any more than Windsor Castle does, it’s about the materialsthat it’s made of and how it performs technically. General rule of thumb forrendering, plastering and pointing: if you have solid walls use a hot-mixnon-hydraulic lime or lime putty, if you have modern cavity walls use cementand gypsum. Natural Hydraulic Lime (NHL) has its place but should be used withcaution as there has been research done by well-respected heritage stonemasons,such as Nigel Copsey that show that over time NHLs can set harder than cement.There are practical examples of this at places like Lincoln Castle where NHLmortars have caused soft stonework to delaminate. If you’re unsure, then steerclear.

But aren’t traditional materials expensive?

Yes and no – An NHL lime would cost about 4x more than a bagof cement, and lime putty can be expensive too, but quicklime for a hot lime mixis comparable to cement when considering it expands to twice it’s volume whenit slakes (slaking is the process when water is added to quicklime and it givesoff heat, hence “hot lime mix”). The main cost comes from the labour as there isa bit of ‘aftercare’ required when working with lime; and considering traditionalskills are harder and harder to come by, this is understandable.

The other thing to consider is that using the propermaterials, you’re unlikely to see the problem again in your lifetime as thematerials should outlast you; if you use the wrong materials then it might onlybe a year until you start getting knock-on problems with damp and mould, whichfurther wastes time and money.

So what about the time it takes?

Working with lime does take longer than using modernmaterials because of the aftercare that is required, which is a real stickingpoint for some customers as this means scaffolding is left up longer forexternal works, or there is more upheaval for internal works. Unfortunately,there isn’t a way around this and it’s why projects need to be carefullyconsidered and phased to mitigate inconvenience to owners, especially if they’reliving in the building during works.

I have seen instances with render where a small area ofrender has failed and run-of-the-mill builders have suggested the whole lotneeds to come off to rerender it in cement. If you get someone in whounderstands the use of a hot-mix lime it’s likely they will be able to patchthe problem area. It would be quicker and cheaper than replacing the whole lotand I can guarantee a modern render would blow again within 20 years (and that’sbeing generous!)

If you want more information, there are a few books I alwaysrecommend. For the complete novice, I’d spend £30 on a copy of the SPAB OldHouse Handbook by Roger Hunt & Marianne Suhr (<https://uk.bookshop.org/p/books/old-house-handbook-a-practical-guide-to-care-and-repair-2nd-edition-roger-hunt/7415760?aid=13434&ean=9780711281479&>)which talks about this and other maintenance issues encountered with an oldhouse in layman’s terms. I’d recommend this book to all old house owners and tothe builders that are working on them; it’s a great reference book beforeapproaching tradesmen or doing your own DIY.

For something a bit more in-depth, homeowners should get a copyof The Warm Dry Home by Pete Ward (<https://shop.heritage-house.org/product/book-the-warm-dry-home/>).I can’t recommend this book enough, it’s a real primer in ensuring that yourold house works as it should throughout the year through all of our GreatBritish seasons.

And if you’re wanting something that’s very technical backedup with a whole lot of research, then get Hot Mixed Lime and TraditionalMortars: A Practical Guide to Their Use in Conservation and Repair by NigelCopsey ([https://www.summerfieldbooks.com/product/hot-mixed-lime-and-traditional-mortars-a-practical-guide-to-their-use-in-conservation-and-repair/?srsltid=AfmBOopAVzmbZMg227ty-_fGwmfEK7_1-8AjKzjOfTfimtFU200bZ2HW](https://www.summerfieldbooks.com/product/hot-mixed-lime-and-traditional-mortars-a-practical-guide-to-their-use-in-conservation-and-repair/?srsltid=AfmBOopAVzmbZMg227ty-_fGwmfEK7_1-8AjKzjOfTfimtFU200bZ2HW))
